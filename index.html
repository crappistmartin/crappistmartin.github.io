Affordable Rent Is Not Social Rent

Mar 10th, 2015 8:37 pm

We blogged previously about the last phase of the Aylesbury redevelopment, where we saw plans waived through as social rent, only to discover afterwards that the s106 legal agreement contained a corrupted definition of the term social rent, which referred to the legal definition of affordable rent (80% market rent).

Notting Hill Housing Group, which is developing the remaining phases of the Aylesbury estate, has taken a different approach: instead of substituting social rent for affordable rents via the back door, it is openly defining affordable rent as a subcategory of social rent. Its consultation document for Manor Place development (a former Council refuse and recycling centre off Walworth road) shows rents for its new social rented homes will be set at “up to 50% of local market rents”. Those familiar with housing policy will know that social rents are not determined by Housing Associations; they are determined by the National Rent Regime regulatory framework - and will know that they are not set according to a percentage of market rents; they are set according to average household incomes.

To some people 50% of market rents may sound like a good deal, but in an area where market rents have rocketed to £300 per week for a 1-bed flat, it represents a 50% increase above social rent levels. The crucial difference being that market rents rise well beyond wage increases; an important consideration in an area where the average household income of a council tenant is £9,100 per year1. Some might see this as an inevitable consequence of the market and beyond the control of public authorities, but this is Council-owned land being sold off to a Housing Association, which could have been used to build much needed truly affordable and truly social rented homes.

Southwark is undertaking a study of the affordability of affordable rent in the borough. This spreadheet from the study shows that the minimum household income required to be able to afford one of Notting Hill’s new 50% market rent ‘social rented’ homes at Manor Place is £39,860 per year - well beyond the £9,100 average household income of council tenants in Walworth and the £21,000 average household income of all residents in Walworth.

Notting Hill are also involved in the Council’s Elmingtion estate regeneration where the council has just issued a Compulsory Purchase Order on 144 homes in phase 3 of the regeneration. Phases 1 & 2, where Notting Hill has already demolished 373 council homes, is nearing completion and the new ‘affordable’ homes are currently being marketed in the development, which has now rebranded ‘Camberwell Fields’.

Paragraph 12 of this Council report confirms that all replacement 1 & 2 bed social housing in the redevelopment will be set at 65% market rent. According to Southwark’s figures this will amount to £194 per week, which is more than double the existing council rents. Residents who have been ‘decanted’ from the estate and wish to exercise their right to return, will therefore need to be able to afford a 100% rent hike.

Furthermore, the main component of the new ‘affordable’ housing on the Elmington is shared ownership. Notting Hill has just released the price list for its new shared ownership homes in the development. This shows that the cheapest of these is a 1-bed flat priced at £380k, for which you will need a minimum 40% share of £152k and a minimum household income of £51,824 in order to pay the £1,438 per month rent, mortgage repayment and service charge; hardly affordable to those currently living in Camberwell, where the average household income is £24,400.

Last week, the latest phase of the Heygate redevelopment went on sale in Hong Kong, coinciding with L&Q’s launch of its shared ownership homes on the redeveloped estate. According to L&Q’s price list, the cheapest of these will set you back £126k for a 30% stake in a 1-bed flat, for which you will need to buy a 30% minimum share, requiring a household income of at least £57,500k per year. A 25% share in a 2-bed duplex in the new ‘affordable’ Heygate will set you back £185,625. With a minimum household income requirement of £64,500 it won’t be long before hard-up Southwark residents will be able to staircase up to full ownership of this duplex at a mere £742,500.


Last week Southwark Council also launched an interactive map inviting people to suggest where it should build the 11,000 new ‘council’ homes it has pledged to build. Rumour has it that the Council is having a little difficulty identifying where to build these homes as vacant land in the borough is limited. In December, the Council even suggested exploring the purchase of land on the open market from developers. Given its track record in negotiatiating with developers, we wonder why none of these new ‘council’ homes could be built on the vast estates that the Council is redeveloping (Heygate, Aylesbury, Wooddene & Elmington etc)? Surely this would be better than knocking down the 5,000 council homes on these sites and selling the land off to developers and corporated-minded Housing Associations?
