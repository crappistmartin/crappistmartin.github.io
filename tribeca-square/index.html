Delancey’s Tribeca Square Development - 0% Affordable Housing

Jan 18th, 2015 1:38 pm

Delancey’s ‘Tribeca Square’ development currently going up next to Elephant & Castle train station, was originally named after TriBeCa – short for Triangle Below Canal Street, which is a neighbourhood in Lower Manhattan. The developers promised to rename the development after protests from local residents.

Whilst the name “Elephant One” has been floated by developers as a possible alternative, a new name has not yet definitively been chosen. The development is being built on the site of what used to be an industrial estate originally owned by the GLA, which used to be leased to a Volvo dealership and several small SMEs.

The site was sold in 2007 to a shell company (Eadon Ltd.) registered in Isle of Man and owned by controversial developer Delancey DV4. Delancey is funded by George Soros, it is managed by Tory Donor Jamie Ritblatt and has instructed former Southwark deputy council leader Kim Humphries to advise on its development.

Affordable housing - or lack thereof

Original planning permission was granted in 2006, but in December 2008 Delancey/Eadon came back to Southwark’s planning committee asking for a variation to its s106 agreement.

Despite adding 61 extra private residential units to the revised scheme, Delancey submitted a viability assessment and argued that because of the economic downturn, the scheme was no longer viable enough to provide any affordable housing or any payment in-lieu of affordable housing.

The planning officer’s report recommended approval of the development, stating that the council’s in-house valuers had confirmed that “the negative effect of on-site social rented housing on the end values of the private units could in this case render the scheme financially unviable”. The report explained that the design implications of providing a “separate entrance for the affordable units would reduce the overall retail provision within the scheme which wouldn’t support the planning objective of creating a strong and continuous retail frontage at the base of the towers.”

Under Southwark’s tariff for in-lieu affordable housing payments this development should have provided a total of £52.7m to the council1. Instead Delancey offered the measly sum of just £1m towards offsite provision of affordable housing as a consolation.

Curiously, we have been able to get our hands on the confidential viability assessment Delancey submitted for this application and have found that some of its estimates were questionable to say the least; the viability assessment significantly underestimated the end sales value of the residential flats, claiming they would sell for an average of just £525 per square foot.

This is well below the £829 per square foot2 average sales price achieved by the neighbouring Strata Tower development. If we take the difference between Delancey’s estimate and the Strata end sales values (£304 psf) and multiply this by the total residential floorspace (247,079 sqft), then we can say that Delancey undervalued its development by a staggering £75 million.

Another way of putting it in context would be to compare Delancey’s estimate against the current sales price of flats on the neighbouring Heygate redevelopment, the cheapest of which is a 1-bed flat (429 sqft) currently on sale for £415,000 - (£967 per sqft). This is nearly double what Delancey estimated its flats would be worth and if we take this figure for our comparison we can say that the viability assessment undervalued the development by as much as £109m (£967-£525 = £442psqft X 247,049 sqft).

The huge discrepancy between estimated and actual end sales values, might be explained to some extent by rising property values in the (sometimes prolonged) period between planning approval and completion. But even time-adjusted comparisons show that the original estimates are extremely pessimistic.

More importantly it highlights the need for viability review mechanisms linked to delivery. Review mechanisms are not just a good idea - they are also recommended in RICS guidance on viability testing3. Unfortunately, councils are neither qualified or experienced in negotiating and enforcing such mechanisms, or they are simply so eager to see something built on empty sites that have been blighting the area for years that they are reluctant to act.

This is a concrete example of how developers are pulling a fast one with viability assessments and how some councils are letting them get away with it. We think that what’s needed is tighter guidance and policy on viability testing, which includes transparency as part of the process.

Interestingly, this is what Islington council is proposing in its recent discussion paper on viability testing. The paper is currently out for consultation with the aim of producing a Supplementary Planning Document setting out formal guidance later this year. We will be keeping a close eye on this interesting policy development and prompting Southwark council to follow Islington’s progressive lead.

  1. Southwark’s in-lieu affordable housing tariff is £100,000 per habitable room x 527 habitable rooms (35% of Tribeca Square total) = £52.7m↩

  2. See Strata Tower achieved end sales values (avg. £829 psqft) on pg. 4 of this Winter 2011/2012 Colliers International market data report.↩

  3. See paragraph 3.6.4 of the RICS Guidance on Viability Testing.↩
