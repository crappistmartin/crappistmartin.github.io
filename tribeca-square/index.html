Ten Facts About Delancey’s Tribeca Square Development

Jan 18th, 2015 1:38 pm

Delancey’s Tribeca Square development was originally named after TriBeCa – short for Triangle Below Canal Street, which is a neighbourhood in Lower Manhattan. The developers promised to rename the development after protests from local people. Whilst the name “Elephant One” has been floated by developers as a possible alternative, a new name has not definitively been chosen. The development is being built on the site of what used to be an industrial estate owned by the GLA, which used to leased to a Volvo dealership and several small SMEs.

The site was sold in 2007 to a Delancey shell company (Eadon Ltd.) registered in Isle of Man for £18m. It is being developed by controversial developer Delancey DV4, managed by Tory Donor Jamie Ritblatt.

Affordable housing - or lack thereof

Original planning permission was granted in 2006, but in December 2008, the developers came back to Southwark’s planning committee asking for a variation to its s106 agreement.

Despite adding 61 extra private residential units to the revised scheme, the developer argued that because of the economic downturn the scheme was no longer viable enough to provide any affordable housing onsite or any payment in-lieu of affordable housing.

The planning officer’s report recommended approval of the development, stating that the council’s in-house valuers had confirmed that “the negative effect of on-site social rented housing on the end values of the private units could in this case render the scheme financially unviable”. The report explained that the design implications of providing a “separate entrance for the affordable units would reduce the overall retail provision within the scheme which wouldn’t support the planning objective of creating a strong and continuous retail frontage at the base of the towers.”

Under Southwark’s tariff for in-lieu affordable housing payments this development should have provided a total of £52.7m to the council1. Instead the council got nothing because the viability assessment submitted by developers said the development wasn’t viable.

Prefabs are back

Bathrooms for the Tribeca Square development are being pre-fabricated in Dubai and delivered to the site ready made.

Sale and leaseback agreement

When Southwark’s Regeneration Agreement with Lend Lease was leaked in 2013, we noticed a clause referring to the Delancey/Eadon development and market square, with the curious title of Eadon Sale and Leaseback Documents. {extract from RA} A little digging around at the Land Registry showed that these documents had been lodged under the title register and were available for the general public to order.

Bronze Elephants

As part of the development’s s106 contributions (i.e. contribution to the public realm), it has commissioned an artist to sculpt a bronze elephant fountain “standing on its hind legs on top of a tall castle tower, in the act of leaping into the air with its water-spraying trunk raised high in triumph.” . The artist’s proposal also included plans for “a series of small elephants which will be distributed as bronze key rings to the owners of the many new apartments associated with the redevelopment.”
