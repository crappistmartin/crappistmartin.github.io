Aylesbury Estate Planning Application - 934 Social Rented Homes to Be Lost

Nov 1st, 2014 7:57 am

After waiting over a month for them to be ‘validated’ by the council, the documents for Aylesbury estate revelopment planning applications are now on-line. . The application for the First Development Site seeks permission to demolish the Bradenham, Chartridge & Chiltern blocks comprising 630 homes and replace them with 815 new homes ({ref:14-AP-3843}). 362 of these new homes will be at ‘target rent’ - a net loss of 204 units (if target rent is equivalent to social rent). The amount of free market housing on this first phase will increase from 64 homes (initially bought under right-to-buy) to 412 and there will also be 102 intermediate units.

The 204 social rented units lost on the first site is a fraction of the total net loss from the Aylesbury regeneration as a whole. The Affordable Housing Statement submitted for the outline application 2,735 new homes - ref:14/AP/3844)) shows that the social rented housing provision will be reduced by over a third, with a net loss of 934 social rented homes across the estate once the regeneration is completed.

Target Rent = affordable rent or social rent?

In the table shown ‘Estate Baseline’ homes are referred to as ‘Target and Affordable Rent’ which they never were; they were council homes at council rents. Using ‘target rent’, ‘affordable rent’ and ‘social rent’ as if they are all the same thing confuses and misleads. What people really want to know is how much total rent will tenants be paying, whatever the housing is called - the Affordable Housing Statement does not say.

There is good reason to worry on this point: as we reported in a previous blog post, the legal agreement for the last phase of the Aylesbury redevelopment, which is currently being built by L&Q Housing Association, contains a definition of ‘social rented’ which refers to the HCA definition of Boris’s ‘affordable rented’ housing product of up to 80% market rents:

Neither is there any reference to the National Rent Regime in the agreement. This is the regulatory framework which decides the level of social rents based - among other things - on the average level of income, and it is standard practice for definitions in these legal agreements to include a reference to the National Rent Regime. Before the legal agreement is signed and published the planning application has to be approved by Southwark’s planning committee; Southwark is also a partner in the regeneration, so it is up to Southwark to ensure that this ‘Phase 1 situation’ is not repeated, so that we don’t end up with ‘social housing’ that is nothing of the kind.

Cycling and car parking

The council recently announced plans for a ‘Southwark Spine’ cycle route linking the south to the north of the borough, which is due to cut straight through the new Aylesbury development1. However, cycle campaign groups have pointed out that the planning applications make no provision for any segregated cycle lanes, instead cyclists will be forced to continue to compete for road space on Thurlow street with buses as the illustrations clearly show. A petition has been set up by cycle campaign group ‘Stop Killing Cyclists’.

Paragraph 2.5 of the Transport Statement also confirms that “the proposals are for 1378 residential car parking spaces across the development”. We think this is excessive in an area with so many public transport links on the fringe of zone 1 London. Environmental Impact

Planning regulations require major redevelopment applications to provide “An outline of the main alternatives studied by the applicant or appellant and an indication of the main reasons for the choice made, taking into account the environmental effects.” This is to ensure that developers have properly considered the environmental impact of their proposals against the possible impact of alternatives and refurbishment. It would appear that no such appraisal or any detailed cost/benefit analysis has been undertaken in relation to the Aylesbury redevelopment. UCL’s Engineering Exchange recently published a detailed report showing that “refurbishment of housing is often preferable to demolition and reconstruction” when taking into account environmental and social factors.

Equalities Impact

Council papers state that “the Aylesbury is a very diverse community with 67% of people belonging to a minority ethnic group”. According to the Equalities Act 2010, the planning application should have included a detailed equalities impact assessment to consider whether the planning proposals will have an adverse effect on any Black and Minority Ethnic(BME) groups. We have been unable to find any such assessment in the supporting documents for either application.

Southwark is suddenly ‘keen to disclose viability assessment’

Southwark’s spin doctors were in full spin this week when they announced that the council is about to disclose the Heygate viability assessment. Having spent two years and a sum running into six figures fighting the Information Commissioner’s order to disclose the document, the council’s regen boss claimed “Southwark Council has always been keen to see this matter resolved as quickly as possible.” Southwark’s sudden keenness has surely got nothing to do with the fact that at a recent Case Management Hearing, the Tribunal judge gave Southwark a deadline of 5th Nov to disclose the viability assessment…
