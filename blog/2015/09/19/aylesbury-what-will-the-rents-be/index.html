Aylesbury - What Will the Rents Be?

Sep 19th, 2015 9:03 am

For anyone still in any doubt about the tenure of the new ‘social’ housing Notting Hill is planning to provide on the redeveloped Aylesbury estate, we have compiled the following summary.

Background

To provide a little background context about the Housing Association behind the redevelopment, this is a quote from Kate Davies - Notting Hill’s Chief Executive Officer:

Quote from Kate Davies, fellow of the CSJ chaired by Iain Duncan Smith

Kate is a fellow of the Centre for Social Justice (CSJ) - a conservative think tank chaired by Iain Duncan Smith. The CSJ was responsible for shaping the government Housing Reforms that introduced affordable rent in 2011, the reforms were shaped in part by this report from a CSJ working group chaired by Kate Davies. In the foreword to the report she claimed that council estates are ‘ghettos of the poorest and neediest people’ that are ‘subsidised by the taxpayer’ and provide ‘low cost living for life funded from the public purse’. She said that tenants ‘often pay little or no rent, and get their home maintained in good order for free’ and concluded that ‘social housing is not a desirable destination’ and that ‘private ownership is preferable to state provided solutions’.

When the unpopular social housing reforms were being pushed through parliament by the conservatives in 2011, Notting Hill Housing was one of the few housing providers to write in support of the proposals. In its official consultation response it wrote: “Notting Hill strongly welcomes the Government’s social housing reforms. They will lead to a significant increase in the provision of new social housing, with the higher levels of income that associations will receive from affordable rent tenancies.”

Bermondsey Spa

Find out what the current status is with Bermondsey Spa? explain what happened and why this raises concerns for Notting Hill’s rent promsies on the Aylesbury.

What is ‘target rent’?

Throughout Notting Hill’s public consultation on its planning application for the Aylesbury, it referred to the replacement social housing neither as social rent or affordable rent, but as ‘target rent’.

We pointed out during the consultation that the term ‘target rent’ has no planning policy support and that the only terms recognised by planning policy are social rent or affordable rent. Our concerns were addressed in the council’s planning report for the application where it claimed that Notting Hill had “clarified that in all cases references to ‘target rent’ are referring to the social rent tenure” and that the council is satisfied because social rent will be secured in the (Section 106) legal agreement.

Paragraph 109 of the Aylesbury application officer’s report

However, two weeks later during the cross examination of Notting Hill’s Director of Regeneration (Rosemary Houseman) at the CPO Public Inquiry, she admitted there is a difference between social rent and target rent, but that it “isn’t a massive difference” (see the end of the following clip).

Funding conditions

During the Inquiry Ms Houseman was also quizzed about the £59m funding Notting Hill had received for the redevelopment from the Mayor’s 2015-18 MHC funding programme. She conceded that this funding programme is linked to the provision of affordable rent not social rent, but argued that it was the ‘capped’ not ‘discounted’ version of affordable rent that would be provided.

The prospectus for the funding programme explains that funding is limited to shared ownership and affordable rent tenure homes and that its funding for affordable rent comes in two flavours: ‘discounted’ which is up to 80% market rent; and ‘capped’ which is capped at 50% market rent.

Extract from 2015-18 MHC Funding Prospectus

Ms Houseman went on to explain that there wouldn’t be a ‘massive difference’ between Notting Hill’s ‘target rents’ and current social rents on the Aylesbury, but claimed that there wasn’t any detail available on what the prospective rents will be.

We have since discovered that Notting Hill submitted prospective rent levels for each and every one of the ‘affordable’ new homes as part of its bid submission for mayoral funding. We have written to Notting Hill requesting details of these prospective rent levels and are yet to receive a reply.

S106 Agreement

We expressed our concerns to the council throughout the public consultation for Notting Hill’s planning application. We complained that there wasn’t a single occurence of the term ‘social rent’ in any of the planning application documents and that the affordable housing statement referred only to ‘target/affordable rent’.

We were nevertheless assured by council officers that there was no need to worry, because social rented tenure would be secured by the Section 106 agreement.

However, last month the council published the Section 106 agreement for the Aylesbury redevelopment, which covers both the First Development Site (FDS - phase one) and the Outline Development Site (the rest of the scheme). We have had a thorough read of the agreement and have raised some serious concerns about the wording, which we think could open the door to interpretation as affordable rent on the Outline Site.

Our main concern is that the agreement’s affordable housing schedule prescribes ‘social rented’ housing for the First Development Site (FDS), but the wording for the Outline Site has omitted the word ‘rented’ and says just ‘social’ housing. Given the fact that there is no definition elsewhere in the agreement for the term ‘Social’, we are worried that any future legal dispute over the interpretation of this term may fall back on the statutory definition, which includes affordable rent under the broad definition of ‘social’ housing in section 68 of the Housing and Regeneration Act 2008.

Schedule 8 of the S106 Agreement

Of additional concern is that various references can be found in the agreement in relation to social rented housing on the FDS, but there are no specific references to social rented housing in relation to the Outline Site:

Schedule 3 of the S106 Agreement

Two weeks ago we wrote a letter to Councillor Williams, setting out our concerns in detail and requesting that the S106 agreement be amended.

We figured that if the council and Notting Hill are genuinely committed to the provision of social rented rather than affordable rented housing on the Aylesbury, then we saw no reason why they wouldn’t agree to these amendments.

We still haven’t yet received a response from Cllr Williams. We also understand that these tenure and rent level concerns are going to be taken up by the Objectors’ counsel at the CPO Public Inquiry, which resumes on 13th & 14th October at Southwark Council’s Tooley St HQ.
