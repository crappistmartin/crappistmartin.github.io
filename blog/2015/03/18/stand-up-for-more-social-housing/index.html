Stand Up for More Social Housing (at 80% Market Rent)

Mar 18th, 2015 9:29 am

Notting Hill Director Kate Davies“Let’s all of us stand up for more social housing” was the call to arms from Notting Hill Housing Trust’s Director Kate Davies at yesterday’s ‘Homes for Britain’ rally. The rally was organised by the National Housing Federation of which NHHT is a founding member.

We have blogged previously about NHHT’s dodgy definition of social housing and its blanket policy of converting all its existing social rented properties to affordable rent when tenancies expire.

NHHT has secured contracts to build 4,700 new homes in Southwark’s development pipeline, so we thought it might be a good idea to take a closer look at the kind of ‘social’ housing Notting Hill’s Director wants us to stand up for more of.

‘The Exchange’ - Notting Hill’s 205 unit development at the Bermondsey Spa regeneration scheme (site 5C) This is ‘The Exchange’ in Bermondsey, Notting Hill’s latest development to be completed in Southwark and part of the Bermondsey Spa regeneration scheme. Notting Hill has sold all but three private and two shared-ownership units in this 205-home development. The remaining private flats are all priced at over £1m and the shared-ownership flats require a minimum salary of £74k to qualify.

Demolition of 54 council homes on the siteThe development was also supposed to include 44 social rented units, to help make up for the 54 council homes that were demolished to make way for it.

In any case that’s what Notting Hill claimed in its planning application for the site and that’s what was confirmed in the planning officer’s report. Even the mayor produced a planning report confirming that the development was proposing 44 social rented homes. However, what was eventually signed off by the council in the s106 legal agreement was something completely different: 44 ‘affordable rented’ units (ie. up to 80% market rents).

Officers report states 44 Social Rented homes but S106 Agreement says Affordable Rented units

The change in wording is subtle but has significant consequences. According to Southwark’s own figures, a 1-bed social rented flat in Bermondsey costs an average £97 per week, compared to £273 per week for ‘affordable rent’ at 80% market rent.

The public have been deceived over this development: they were led to believe they were getting social rents at planning approval stage, then a ‘switch’ to affordable rent was effected when the legal documents were drawn up after approval was granted.

We showed in our last blog how this sleight-of-hand is becoming commonplace in Southwark’s planning process. It’s a convenient arrangement that allows the council and Notting Hill to trumpet the building of social housing and oppose affordable rent in public, while allowing it in practice.

Extract from Aylesbury estate Development AgreementWe have shown in previous blog posts how Notting Hill is using the terms ‘social’, ‘target’ and ‘affordable’ rent interchangably in its forthcoming application for the Aylesbury estate redevelopment. We also blogged about the worrying fact that affordable rents of up to 80% are included in its development agreement for the Aylesbury site.

Notting Hill’s Director & Southwark council leader celebrate demolition of Aylesbury redevelopment’s phase 2 Will the Aylesbury go the same way as the Bermondsey Spa regeneration with affordable rent slipped in quietly through the back door? The answer is we won’t know until after the planning application has been decided. It can take several months for the s106 legal agreement to be drafted and we won’t know what definition it will contain until it has been signed.

In the meantime we need to let the council know that their game is up and that we are not going to accept this disingenuous racket. The Aylesbury planning application is due to be heard by committee on 14th April and we will be making our voices heard. We invite you to support us by objecting to the planning application online using our automated objection form.
