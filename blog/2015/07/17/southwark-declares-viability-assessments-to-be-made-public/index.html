Southwark Declares Viability Assessments to Be Made Public

Jul 17th, 2015 3:21 pm

Neo (jerry)

Our favourite development north of the borough, has been shortlisted for the prestigious ‘Sterling architecture award’. This horrible looking building on the doorstep of the Tate Gallery, delivered 0% on-site affordable housing despite being in one of the highest value locations in London. In 2006, developer Native Land successfully argued (read ‘bamboozled’) first, that the 40% affordable housing requirement was too high with the aid of a viability assessment and that it could only afford 27.5%, and secondly, that it would be better built elsewhere, on land that Native Land would acquire.

This off-site housing would comprise intermediate homes and 94 social rented units. Things haven’t worked out that way and in March this year Southwark signed off) on the affordable housing contributions when only 62 social rented units have been secured of which only 55 have been built. These are the sites with the respective number of social rented units provided by Native Land (the information comes from the s106 agreements):

Lost social housing

We can add the 32 lost homes to the 44 lost Bermondsey Spa social rented homes and wonder how many other developers are also failing to deliver the social rented housing required by the conditions of their planning permissions.

We also wonder what Southwark is doing about this. An audit is required to see how widespread this practice is.

Neo Bankside

Paragraph 97 of the officer’s report for the application states that a total of 94 social rented units must be provided by the developer off-site:

The terms of the Neo Bankside s106 agreement further stipulate, that these off-site affordable housing units must be handed over to a housing association and the tenure secured for a least 125 years via individual s106 agreements for each site.

In January this year, developer Native land submitted an application to the council, asking it to fully discharge the planning application’s s106 off-site affordable housing requirements (i.e. sign off the off-site affordable housing requirement as fulfilled).

On Thursday the Guardian journalist Olly Wainwright revealed that Neo Bankside had been shortlisted for the Sterling Prize architecture award. Olly criticised the development for having not only having evaded affordable housing policy by submitting a very questionable viability assessment, but that the provision of the small amount of off-site social housing hadn’t yet been provided.

Gary Rice responded has been delivered and is occupied.) in protest to the article, claiming that it was “not true” and that “all the offsite affordable (social rented) has been delivered and is occupied.”

We followed up by asking for a schedule of all the offsite affordable housing provision. Gary responded by sending us a link to a press release on the developer’s website.

We noted that the developer’s figures didn’t match ours and asked Gary if the council relied on such press releases when it came to application for discharging the planning obligations.

He assured us that the council did have its own schedule supporting its decision to sign off the discharge and promised to upload it to the planning portal. It is still yet to appear but were told it would be uploaded “in due course” and that “patience is a virtue”.

For us this an important issue. We have blogged a great deal about the serious discrepancies that are appearing, between what gets agreed at planning committee and what actually gets delivered on the ground. This schedule underpinned the council’s March 2015 decision to sign off Neo Bankside’s affordable housing obligations as fully delivered. It should have gone through and checked the S106 agreements for each of the sites, ensuring that the affordable units are secured in the right number and under the correct tenure mix.

If the public is unable to verify that this high-profile development has delivered on its affordable housing promises as it claims, then what faith can local people have in the planning process?

With the public benefits of this development being so widely broadcast, it is worth noting that four of the sites used for off-site provision were council owned land, sold to Native Land specifically for the purpose. Furthermore, one of these used to be a council-run Children’s home and site 3 was a council-run day nursery - neither of which have been re-provided. In addition, part of site 5 (Long Lane) is being built on the Elim estate children’s play area.

Summary: Neo Bankside comprises 233 private homes. According to policy, 40% of these should have been affordable - 70% of which should have been social rented. Due to the viability assessment, it was allowed to mitigate by building 94 social rented units off-site, but only 51 have been secured and only 44 completed to date.
