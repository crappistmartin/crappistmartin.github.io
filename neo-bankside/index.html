Neo Bankside

Jul 15th, 2015 9:30 am

In 2006 developers submitted a planning application for the Richard Rogers Neo Bankside development, accompanied by a viability assessment claiming that the development was unable to provide the 40% affordable housing required on-site by policy (which should have been split 70:30 - [70% social rented:30% shared ownership]).

The officer’s report claimed that “Financial appraisals have been submitted to demonstrate that the financial viability of the development is insufficient to deliver the requisite amount of affordable housing on site. The financial appraisals have been scrutinised by a Council Valuer who is in broad agreement with the applicant’s conclusions that the requisite proportion of affordable housing cannot be delivered on site.” (para 92)

Instead the developer offered what was equivalent to 27.5% affordable housing, with the shared ownership provided on-site and the social rented element to be provided by the developer off-site within the Borough & Bankside area:

“The off-site provision would be provided by development of other sites within the Borough and Bankside Community Council area .. a schedule of shortlisted sites has been submitted by the applicant.” (para 95)

Paragraphs 93 & 97 of the officer’s report explains that the agreed affordable housing offer was as follows:

  • £1m commuted sum.

  • 32 shared ownership units would be provided on-site.

  • 130 units would be provided off-site, comprising: 36 shared ownership and 94 social rented units.

In addition, the report said that “The terms of the S106 agreement, which are still being finalised, require the developer to acquire sites in the Borough and Bankside Community Council area, to seek planning permission for residential developments, and the make those affordable housing units available in parallel with the private units being provided on the main site.” (para 108)

However, since the original s106 agreement was signed for this planning permission, the developer came back six times asking for variations to it. This started with an application to extend the boundary for off-site provision beyond the Borough & Bankside area - the council subsequently allowed 75% of the total to be provided elsewhere in the borough (one of the sites was on the Old Kent rd in Peckham); then they applied to remove the s106 agreement’s restrictions on occupation of the Neo Bankside flats before the off-site provision had been completed; then they applied to be able to buy flats on the open market and convert them to affordable housing instead of building the units.

In the last variation (May 2014) they came back asking to make a payment in-lieu of the outstanding off-site provision requirement for the wider area(i.e. part of the 75%), claiming that they had been unable to find any suitable development sites/RSL partners elsewhere in the borough.

The officer’s report for this variation further confirms that “there is an outstanding balance of 3,747 sqft to be provided within the BCCA” (i.e. part of the overall 25% still to be provided within the Borough & Bankside area) and that “The outstanding requirement in relation to the provision of AH within the BCCA remains unresolved as yet”.

Paragraph 77 of the officer’s report also says that the viability assessment justified an abnormally high number of parking spaces (40% of the flats have parking) in what is supposed to be a car-free development zone. To accommodate this, the Neo Bankside development was built with a car-stacking apparatus and a huge two-level car park basement underneath the site.

In 2014, when the council accidentally uploaded it as part of its migration to new planning portal software, we managed to obtain a copy of the viability assessment that justified these significant departures from policy. We subsequently blogged about how the viability assessment had assumed an incredibly pessimistic estimate of the end sales values of the completed appartments (£754 per sqft, compared to the actual sales value achieved - £1,326 per sqft).

As with the Heygate viability assessment, the developer’s assumptions were readily accepted by the council and this time the council didn’t even subject it for third party appraisal - it was officers who interrogated the figures.

Also, as with the Heygate application, officers didn’t attach any kind of review mechanism that would capture increasing property values over the 8 year period between planning and completion.
